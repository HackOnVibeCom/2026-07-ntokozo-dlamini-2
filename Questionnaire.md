# Questionnaire — Zink

## What does your application/service do

Zink is a multi-agent launch orchestrator for mobile apps. It takes a freshly launched mobile app and autonomously plans, writes, designs, and projects a complete, downloadable launch campaign in a single pass. Five specialized agents — a Planner, a Copywriter + Community specialist, a Designer, an Analyst, and a System Orchestrator — execute sequentially through a typed state machine, each handling a distinct phase of the launch workflow. The Planner produces a 14-day strategy and channel mix; the Copywriter generates an App Store listing, an X thread, an Instagram caption, a launch email, and a Reddit/HN angle; the Designer creates a brand colour palette and a declarative SVG poster (no image API required); the Analyst runs a transparent heuristic install projection based on the budget and category. The entire output — strategy document, copy assets, visual poster, and projection numbers — is bundled into a single launch kit that the user can download as a Markdown file. Zink runs fully offline with no API keys needed, using a deterministic mock provider by default, or optionally connects to open-source LLMs via NVIDIA NIM when online. The application streams agent reasoning live to the browser via Server-Sent Events, giving the user a real-time view of the campaign being built.

## Who is the target audience

The primary audience is indie mobile app developers and small startup teams who need to launch a mobile app but lack a dedicated marketing department, the budget for an agency, or the time and expertise to plan a multi-channel launch campaign from scratch. This includes solo developers, two-person founding teams, students publishing their first app, and small studios releasing on the App Store and Google Play. These users typically have a working app but no structured go-to-market plan. They need something that takes their app's core details and turns them into a credible, actionable launch kit without requiring marketing experience. A secondary audience is hackathon participants and prototyping teams who need to produce a polished-looking launch narrative quickly as part of a pitch or demo. The tool is designed for non-marketers — the brief form asks for only six inputs (app name, audience, category, store URL, budget, and design preference), and the result is a complete campaign with no further configuration.

## Which countries are the expected buyers of this service

The primary market is English-speaking countries with large indie developer communities: the United States, the United Kingdom, Canada, Australia, and New Zealand. These markets have the highest concentration of solo and small-team app developers, the most active app store ecosystems, and the strongest culture of paid tools for developers. Secondary markets include South Africa, India, Nigeria, Kenya, and other emerging mobile-first economies where app development is growing rapidly but access to affordable launch marketing tools is limited. In these markets, the offline-first design and zero subscription cost are particular advantages — users can run the full pipeline without a reliable internet connection. A paid SaaS version could eventually expand to Brazil, Indonesia, and Southeast Asia through localisation. The hackathon origin (HackOnVibe, July 2026) and the developer's location in South Africa mean the initial go-to-market would focus on African and global indie developer communities where the offline reliability and zero-cost model resonate most.

## Who are your competitors

The competitive landscape spans several categories. Full-service mobile app marketing agencies (such as AppLaunch, Gummicube, and Robot Kitty) offer comprehensive launch campaigns but charge thousands of dollars per project — out of reach for indie developers. ASO and app intelligence platforms (Sensor Tower, App Annie/data.ai, AppRadar, AppFollow) provide analytics, keyword tracking, and review monitoring but do not generate launch strategies, copy, or design assets. AI copywriting tools (Jasper, Copy.ai, Writesonic) can produce marketing text but lack app-specific templates, strategic planning, and multi-channel orchestration. Design tools (Canva, Adobe Express) offer poster templates but require manual assembly and produce raster images, not declarative SVGs. No-code marketing platforms (Mailchimp, Hootsuite, Buffer) cover email and social scheduling but do not handle strategy creation or asset generation. The closest conceptual competitor is a human marketing generalist or a junior agency — someone who can take a brief and return a strategy, copy, and visuals. The key difference is that Zink does this autonomously, deterministically, and in under a minute, with no human in the loop.

## What is your advantage

Zink's primary advantage is its fully offline, zero-dependency operation. The deterministic mock provider uses a 20-line PRNG seeded from the brief (FNV-1a hash + Mulberry32) to produce varied but reproducible campaigns without any API call, internet connection, or paid subscription. This means the demo never fails, the output is always valid, and the tool is usable in environments with no connectivity — a judges' room, a plane, a rural area. When online, Zink connects to open-source LLMs via NVIDIA NIM's free tier (meta/llama-3.1-8b-instruct), avoiding the per-call costs of proprietary APIs.

The second advantage is the integrated multi-agent pipeline. Competitors typically solve one part of the launch problem — copy, design, or analytics — and require the user to stitch results together manually. Zink runs five agents in sequence through a typed state machine (INITIALIZE → STRATEGIZE → COPYWRITING → ASSET_GENERATION → PROJECTION → COMPLETE), each building on the previous agent's output, and emits a single bundled launch kit. The user enters six fields and clicks one button.

The third advantage is the architectural design for hackathon and pitch environments. Live agent streaming via SSE creates a "swarm" visual that demonstrates the system is working in real time. The SVG poster is declarative — it generates as a string template rather than a binary image, making it inspectable, editable, and free of image-API dependencies. The projection engine uses transparent heuristics that the user can understand and adjust, not a black-box model. The entire application is a single Next.js process with zero native dependencies, running on a single `npm install && npm run dev`. For a hackathon project and its future as a real product, these design choices mean Zink is dependable to demo, cheap to run, and easy to extend.
